Reading: #WordJournal

Word: Navigation

This reading can be characterized by the word navigation because it explains the aspects of navigating through a web page, and the do's/dont's of the process. The web navigation process starts with a user normally trying to find something. They decide whether to ask first or browse first. If they choose to browse, they make their way through a hierarchy, using signs to guide them. Eventually, if they can't find what they're looking for, they leave. This highlights the unbearable lightness of browsing: there's no sense of scale, direction, or location. The purpose of navigation is to help us find what we're looking for, tell us where we are, tell us what's here, tell us how to use the site, and give us confidence in the people who built it. This is done by using common web navigation conventions such as sections, site location indicators, page names, local navigation, and more. You can test if a site is easy to navigate by using the trunk test and asking these questions: What site is this? What page am I on? What are the major sections of this site? What are my options at this level? Where am I in the scheme of things? How can I search? These concepts are important for me to understand in my career because I am aspiring to become a software engineer, specifically working in the web space, so I need to know these web navigation rules in order to create the best possible websites for my users.
